Conformal mapping triangle meshes#

Not every surface worth latticing exists as CAD. Scanned geometry, an STL region, or a patch generated by your own script arrives as triangle meshes — vertices and triangles, with no native surface coordinates. TriangleMeshSurface gives such a patch the coordinates it lacks: it flattens the patch into a 2D chart and then behaves exactly like a CAD face, so mm.cell_map_from_surface(...) and mm.cell_map_between_surfaces(...) accept it anywhere the CAD guide uses a CADFace. No CadQuery installation is required.

surface = pv.TriangleMeshSurface(vertices, triangles)
cell_map = mm.cell_map_from_surface(surface, cells=(12, 12, 1), height=4.0)
root = mm.gyroid(cell_map, wall_thickness=0.7)

vertices is a list of pv.Vec3 and triangles a list of index triples — the same indexed arrays SurfaceMesh exposes for imported models.

What the patch must be#

The patch must be an open disk: one connected piece of surface with one open boundary and no holes or handles, wound consistently. In practice that means the top of a part, a scanned region, or a height-field strip qualifies; a closed solid, an annulus, or two separate pieces do not (each is rejected with a specific error — see Troubleshooting). A closed solid becomes usable by extracting one open region from it, shown below.

The patch’s shape is unrestricted. Circular, elongated, off-center, and irregular outlines all work — the boundary does not need to be square, straight-edged, or convex.

Two practical freedoms follow:

  • Resolution is independent of cells. A 14 × 14-vertex patch can drive a (12, 10, 1) cell map; a 100 × 100-vertex scan can drive a (6, 6, 2) one. Mesh density controls how faithfully the map follows the surface, not how many cells you get.

  • Winding sets the height direction. The triangle winding defines the outward normal, and a positive height extrudes along it. If a map grows the wrong way, flip the winding or negate height. Normals are smoothed across triangle edges, so the extrusion direction never steps at a facet boundary.

How the patch becomes a chart#

The flattening is near-conformal and keeps the patch’s natural outline: a circular patch flattens to a round chart, an elongated strip to an elongated chart. The chart sits inside its bounding rectangle, reported by surface.uv_bounds with the longer side normalized to 1 and the aspect ratio preserved — so cells stay near-square on the surface instead of being stretched onto a forced square domain.

Flattening preserves angles, not areas. Wall thickness and beam radius remain world-unit controls everywhere, but on strongly curved patches (a deep pocket, a nearly closed cap) cell sizes will vary across the surface. The map’s built-in validation is the authoritative check that the result is still well-formed.

Trimming to the true boundary#

A non-rectangular chart cannot fill its bounding rectangle, and the map does not pretend it does. Every cell is sampled against the chart, and cells that lie entirely in the empty margin between the chart outline and the rectangle are deactivated: they hold no lattice, contribute no geometry, and the mapped lattice terminates at the patch’s real edge. For a circular patch, the corners of the cell grid are simply trimmed away.

The dome's round chart in its UV rectangle: hatched corner cells are trimmed
trim_to_surface=False: corner cells extrapolate past the round edge
trim_to_surface=True (default): the lattice ends at the real boundary

Trimming is on by default (trim_to_surface=True on cell_map_from_surface). Passing False keeps the full rectangular grid; cells beyond the boundary then continue the surface’s own extension, which is occasionally useful for stock that will be cut back later but is rarely what you want. When two surfaces are paired with cell_map_between_surfaces(...), a cell survives only where it lies on both patches, so the interpolated volume is trimmed to their common footprint.

Trimmed maps report how much was removed: cell_map.validate().trimmed_element_count.

Choosing the chart orientation#

The chart’s U axis becomes the lattice’s first logical direction — it decides which way beams, plates, and grading fields run on the surface. Two options:

  • Default: U aligns with the patch’s long direction (its principal axis). An elongated strip automatically gets U along its length; near-round patches keep a deterministic solver orientation.

  • Explicit: pass u_axis_hint=pv.Vec3(...) to any constructor. The world-space direction is projected onto the patch and U is rotated to follow it.

surface = pv.TriangleMeshSurface(vertices, triangles, u_axis_hint=pv.Vec3(1.0, 0.0, 0.0))

Use the hint whenever the lattice’s grain matters — and always when pairing a mesh with a CAD face, as shown below.

Map a procedural patch#

The procedural examples build patches with the helpers in _triangle_surface_examples.py — disk_surface(...) for round patches, grid_surface(...) / indexed_grid(...) for rectangular ones — then map a lattice exactly like a CAD face:

import math

import pyvcad as pv
import pyvcad_metamaterials as mm
import pyvcad_rendering as viz

from _triangle_surface_examples import disk_surface

RADIUS = 15.0

def spherical_cap(x, y):
    r2 = (x * x + y * y) / (RADIUS * RADIUS)
    return 8.0 * math.sqrt(max(0.0, 1.0 - 0.75 * r2))

surface = disk_surface(RADIUS, 10, 36, spherical_cap)
cell_map = mm.cell_map_from_surface(surface, cells=(12, 12, 1), height=4.0)
root = mm.gyroid(cell_map, wall_thickness=0.7)

viz.Render(root)

Select a region of an imported mesh#

Real imported meshes are usually closed solids with several face groups, not a single disk. 21_imported_triangle_mesh_patch.py imports a domed tile — a closed solid with a domed top, four sides, and a flat bottom — then works entirely with SurfaceMesh’s indexed vertices and triangles arrays through the helpers in _triangle_surface_examples.py:

  1. select_triangles(...) keeps the upward-facing top face group (normal.z > 0.5).

  2. largest_connected_component(...) isolates the largest edge-connected patch.

  3. reindex(...) compacts it to its own vertices.

The extracted top is a clean disk, so it is handed straight to TriangleMeshSurface, and the example grades the gyroid’s wall thickness across the patch with a cell_field. The chart keeps the patch’s real outline, so the mapped lattice is trimmed to the region’s true boundary — the selection does not need to be square, or even convex.

Imported solid
Selected top face group
Mapped gyroid

Pick the extraction criterion to match how the region is bounded — a normal threshold works when the region meets its neighbours at a discernible edge, and largest_connected_component(...) then drops any stray triangles that share the same orientation elsewhere on the mesh. What matters is that the selection is a valid disk: one connected component, one boundary loop.

Pair a mesh with a CAD surface#

Because CADFace and TriangleMeshSurface share the ParametricSurface contract, cell_map_between_surfaces(...) fills the volume between any pair of them. 22_between_mesh_and_cad_surfaces.py interpolates an octet lattice between a lower boundary supplied as a triangle mesh — an undulating egg-crate floor — and an upper boundary authored as a smooth CAD dome offset above it. Logical W runs from the mesh at w = 0 to the CAD surface at w = 1.

When one boundary is a mesh chart and the other a CAD face, make sure their U axes agree in world space. The factory’s auto-alignment only searches flips and a U/V exchange, so it corrects mismatches of 90° steps — an intermediate rotation (the egg-crate floor’s default principal-axis chart runs diagonally, 45° off the dome’s U) would twist the whole shell. Pinning the mesh chart with u_axis_hint to the CAD face’s U direction keeps the pair rotationally in step:

floor_surface = pv.TriangleMeshSurface(                                   # lower: triangle mesh
    floor_vertices, floor_triangles, u_axis_hint=pv.Vec3(1.0, 0.0, 0.0)
)
engineered_surface = pv.CADModel.from_cadquery(engineered_cad).faces[0]   # upper: CAD patch

cell_map = mm.cell_map_between_surfaces(floor_surface, engineered_surface, cells=(12, 12, 3))
root = mm.octet(cell_map, beam_radius=mm.cell_field(cell_map, 0.65))
Input boundaries: mesh floor (blue) and CAD dome (orange)
Octet lattice interpolated between them

The two boundaries must share a compatible parameterization and periodicity for the interpolation to stay valid — patches over a common footprint align cleanly, whereas mixing a periodic CAD cylinder with a non-periodic mesh patch is rejected.

Once constructed, these mapped lattices are ordinary OpenVCAD nodes. They can carry the graded fields from the previous guide, participate in heterogeneous lattices, and can be sent to any compatible compiler.

Limitations and troubleshooting#

Rejected input topology#

Each invalid input is rejected with a distinct RuntimeError rather than silently repaired:

Input

Error

Closed solid (a sphere)

“the mesh is closed and has no boundary loop”

Annulus / two boundary loops

“requires exactly one boundary loop”

Punctured torus (a handle)

“requires a genus-0 disk … carries at least one handle”

Two disconnected disks

“requires a single connected patch”

Three triangles on one edge

“has a non-manifold edge”

Two fans joined at a point

“has a non-manifold vertex”

All of these are fixed by extracting or repairing the patch before construction — for closed solids and multi-region meshes, use the region-selection workflow.

When the flattening cannot keep the natural outline#

Some patches cannot be flattened without overlapping themselves — a helical strip sweeping more than a full turn is the canonical case. Instead of rejecting such a patch, TriangleMeshSurface automatically switches to a fallback parameterization that maps the boundary onto a circle. The fallback is always valid but carries more shape distortion, so cell sizes become less uniform, and the chart outline is no longer the patch outline (nothing is trimmed). Check which chart is in use with:

surface.parameterization_method   # "free_boundary_lscm" or "convex_border_mean_value"

If a patch you expected to flatten cleanly reports the fallback, the patch is usually more curved or twisted than intended — splitting it into flatter regions restores the natural-outline chart. Only when both parameterizations fail is the patch rejected, with both reasons in the message.

Map validation errors#

Producing a valid chart does not guarantee the extruded or interpolated volume is valid; construction always runs CellMap’s Jacobian validation:

  • “Surface CellMap is folded or singular” — the height is too large for the surface’s concave curvature (the offset self-intersects), or a fallback chart’s distortion made cells collapse at the requested resolution. Reduce height, add cells, or split the patch.

  • “No valid paired-surface correspondence was found” — the two surfaces of a between_surfaces pair cannot be aligned by flips/exchange, or their charts shear against each other; the message lists the Jacobian of every candidate tried. Surfaces over a common footprint pair cleanly; also check both normals point along the intended W direction.

Other limits to keep in mind#

  • A twisted shell between a mesh and a CAD face usually means the two U axes disagree by an angle the discrete auto-alignment cannot fix — pin the mesh chart with u_axis_hint (see the pairing section).

  • Sharp creases are rounded by the smoothed vertex normals; a patch containing a hard edge extrudes with a small fillet-like transition there. Split at creases when that matters.

  • Severe area distortion (deep pockets, nearly closed caps) leaves cells uneven even when everything validates — inspect cell_map.validate()’s aspect-ratio diagnostics if uniformity matters.
